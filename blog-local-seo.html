The local SEO checklist every small business should run this month

You don't need an agency to start winning local searches — you need a checklist and a free afternoon. Here are the seven local SEO tasks with the highest payoff for small businesses, in the order we run them for clients.

JB

Jordan Blake
Head of SEO, Ignite Studio

Local SEO is the highest-leverage marketing most small businesses aren't doing. Industry analyses consistently estimate that nearly half of all Google searches carry local intent — someone looking for a product, service, or place nearby. And according to BrightLocal's Local Consumer Review Survey, 87% of consumers used Google to evaluate local businesses in the past year. If you're not deliberately competing for those searches, you're conceding them.

The good news: local SEO rewards consistency over budget. Run this checklist and you'll be ahead of most competitors in your zip code.

1. Claim and complete your Google Business Profile

Your Google Business Profile (GBP) is the single most valuable local asset you have — it powers the map pack, the knowledge panel, and 'near me' results. Claim it, verify it, and fill in every field: services, attributes, hours (including holidays), photos, and a keyword-aware but human business description. Businesses with complete profiles are dramatically more likely to be considered reputable by searchers, and Google's own help documentation confirms completeness affects local ranking.

Then treat it like a channel, not a listing: post weekly updates, add fresh photos monthly, and answer the Q&A section before a stranger answers it for you.

2. Nail your NAP consistency

NAP stands for Name, Address, Phone — and inconsistency is the silent killer of local rankings. If your website says "Rooted Coffee Co.," Yelp says "Rooted Coffee Company," and an old directory lists your previous address, Google's confidence in your data drops, and with it your map visibility. Audit the top 20 directories for your exact business name, address format, and phone number. Fix every mismatch. It's tedious, and it works.

3. Build a review engine, not a review wish

Reviews are a top-tier local ranking factor and the first thing customers read. But hoping for reviews isn't a system. Build one: a short link or QR code at the counter, a follow-up text after every job, and a script your team actually uses ("If we earned it, a Google review really helps a small shop like ours"). Velocity and recency matter more than a single big number — a steady two reviews a week beats fifty reviews from 2023.

Respond to every review, positive and negative. Responses are visible trust signals to the next hundred readers, and they signal an active business to Google.

4. Create real location and service pages

One homepage can't rank for every service in every suburb. Create a dedicated page for each core service, and — if you serve multiple areas — for each priority location. The key word is real: a genuine page with local proof, photos, testimonials from that area, and specific answers, not a find-and-replace template. Thin doorway pages violate Google's guidelines; useful local pages dominate map-adjacent searches.

5. Make your site embarrassingly fast on a phone

Local searches happen on phones, often with intent to act within the hour. Google's research with SOASTA found that as mobile load time goes from one second to three, the probability of a bounce increases by 32% — and it climbs steeply from there. Compress images, kill unused scripts, and test your site on a real phone over cellular data, not office Wi-Fi. If it feels slow to you, it's costing you customers. (Our web design service treats speed as a feature, not an optimization.)

6. Add local business schema markup

Schema is structured data that tells search engines — and increasingly AI assistants — exactly what your business is: your hours, services, area, and reviews in machine-readable form. LocalBusiness schema with accurate NAP, plus Service and FAQ markup on the right pages, makes you eligible for richer results and easier for AI search tools to cite. It's an hour of technical work with years of payoff; it's included in every SEO plan we run.

7. Earn a handful of local links

You don't need hundreds of backlinks to win a neighborhood — you need a relevant dozen. Sponsor the little league team, join the chamber of commerce, get listed by the local news roundup, partner with adjacent businesses ("best coffee near the yoga studio"). Each local link is both a ranking signal and a real referral channel. This is marketing your competitors find too unglamorous to do, which is exactly why it works.

Run it monthly, not once

Local SEO isn't a project you finish; it's a rhythm you keep. Profiles drift, listings decay, review velocity stalls, and competitors copy what works.
